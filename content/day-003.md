## Explanation

1.1 said what the world is made of: facts, not things. 1.11 says what makes
those facts add up to a world at all — and the emphasis falls on a single
small word Wittgenstein italicizes: *alle*, all.

"The world is determined by the facts" restates 1.1 — nothing new yet. The
second half is the new claim: "and by these being *all* the facts." A world
is not fixed merely by which facts hold. It is fixed by which facts hold
*and there being no more of them to specify*. Tell someone a hundred true
facts about a room and they still cannot picture it completely; something
could always turn out to be missing. Only the complete list — all of them,
no remainder — pins the room down. Determinacy, for Wittgenstein, is a
property of totalities, not of any accumulation short of one.

This is quiet groundwork for the next two sentences. 1.12 will point out
that a totality of facts fixes not only what *is* the case but what is
*not* — a complete inventory rules things out as much as it rules things
in. 1.11 is what makes that later move available: only because the world
is *all* the facts can their totality carry information about everything
they leave out.

## Method

No Zen note today. The pull here is toward totality-and-interpenetration
language — Indra's net, where each thing is what it is only in relation to
the whole net. But that resonance actually belongs to 1.1's facts-not-things
point, which I already spent yesterday's note on; borrowing it again for
1.11 would be reaching for a parallel to fill the section rather than
because 1.11 itself demands it. 1.11's real content — that determinacy
requires completeness, not mere accumulation — is a point about logical
form, not about a way of seeing the world, and I don't have an honest
counterpart for that narrower claim. Omitting is the correct call, not a
default I'm defaulting to out of laziness: I held it against the doubt test
in METHOD.md and it didn't pass.

No pressure on presentation today: still one thesis, one page, prose
carries the "all" emphasis without needing typographic tricks.
