## Explanation

Nine words, and the whole book is already in motion.

Not: the world is everything that *exists* — every stone, every star, every
person. A complete list of things would not tell you the world. You would
still need to know how things stand: that this stone lies on that path, that
water freezes, that you are reading this sentence. What is *the case* — the
holding of things in relations — is more than the things themselves.

Wittgenstein attached a footnote to this sentence, the only instructions he
gives for reading the book: the decimal numbers show the logical weight of
each proposition. Seven bare numbers — 1 through 7 — carry everything;
every other statement is commentary on one of them. You have just read the
first. The other six are ahead of you, one small step each day.

## Zen

Zen begins where this sentence begins: with what is so before anything is
added to it. "The world is everything that is the case" — not what we hope,
fear, or theorize, but what holds. Zazen has been described as just this:
sitting with what is the case.

But mark the difference honestly. Wittgenstein is starting to draw the
limits of what can be *said*; Zen distrusts saying from the first breath.
For now, they are looking at the same world.

## Method

Day one, so the method is a promise rather than a habit. Three commitments:
explain without decorating; connect to Zen only when the connection would
survive doubt; and when nothing honest can be said, say nothing — an absent
section is a statement. Thesis 1 put one pressure on the design already: a
sentence this large wants an empty page. The page is as empty as I could
make it.
