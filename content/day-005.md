## Explanation

Four sentences in, and the opening run closes here. 1 said the world is
everything that is the case. 1.1 said it is made of facts, not things.
1.11 said it is fixed by being *all* the facts. 1.12 said that totality
fixes what's excluded as much as what holds. 1.13 restates the whole
claim in one line — and adds a single word none of the others used:
*space*.

"The facts in logical space are the world." Not just "the facts are the
world" — 1.1 already said that. The facts, *located somewhere*, are the
world. That somewhere is not a place you could point to on a map; it is
what Wittgenstein calls logical space, and 1.13 does nothing more than
assert that it exists and that the facts sit inside it. What logical
space actually is — how it is built, what else besides the actual facts
belongs to it — is not explained here. That is chapter 2's job, starting
at 2.013, and it runs for the rest of the book.

Still, the last three sentences have already earned this one. 1.12 said
a totality of facts fixes what is excluded, not only what holds. Ask why
exclusion should fall out of totality at all, and the honest answer
needs something for the facts to be excluded *from* — a wider field of
ways things could have been, of which the facts that actually hold are
only some. That wider field is what "logical space" names. A single true
fact, like one lit window on a dark street, tells you something only
because there is a whole street of unlit windows around it that could
have been lit instead and are not. Logical space is the street; the
facts are the windows that happen to be lit.

1.13 does not build the street. It only tells us, for the first time,
that the windows were never floating free.

## Zen

Once built out from 2.013 on, logical space turns out to be fixed in
advance and complete: every possible arrangement of objects is already
there, waiting, whether or not anything ever makes it actual. Which
windows are lit changes night to night; the street itself — every window
that could ever be lit — does not.

Buddhist dependent origination has no equivalent standing behind the
arising world. It does not describe things being drawn from a
pre-existing menu of possibilities; it describes phenomena conditioning
each other into being, with no fixed prior space of what-could-be for
them to occupy first. There is no street of empty windows waiting before
the chain of causes lights the ones that light.

This is a different claim than 1.1's Zen note made, though it draws on
the same Buddhist concept: 1.1 asked whether facts outrank things as what
the world is *made of* — a question about parts. 1.13 asks whether there
is a fixed totality of possibility sitting *behind* the facts, prior to
and independent of which of them happen to obtain — a question about a
container. Dependent origination answers both, but not by repeating
itself; it denies a container the same way it denies fixed parts. Worth
keeping for the second, sharper thing it shows about how strange, and
how quietly Western, "logical space" already is this early.
