## Explanation

1.11 said the world needs *all* the facts, not just some. 1.12 says why
completeness is worth wanting: a totality of facts settles two things at
once, not one.

The first is obvious — which facts are true, what *is* the case. The
second is easy to miss: which facts are *not* true, what is *not* the
case. Wittgenstein's claim is that a single totality fixes both, and it
fixes the second only because it fixes the first completely.

Think of a guest list posted at the door: "everyone who has arrived." If
the list is genuinely complete, it does two jobs at once. It tells you
who is here. It also tells you, without a single word about them, who is
not — anyone missing from the list is thereby excluded. A partial list
could never do this: someone absent from an incomplete list might simply
not have been written down yet, still on their way in. Only completeness
lets absence-from-the-list mean absence-from-the-party.

That is the whole content of 1.12, and it sounds almost too simple to
state — but it is doing real work. It is the first appearance, however
quiet, of *exclusion* as a feature of the world itself, not of language.
What is ruled out is ruled out by the very same totality that fixes what
holds, not by some separate operation applied afterward. The technical
machinery for negation as a logical operation is chapters away —
Wittgenstein does not even use the word here. But the shape of the idea,
that a complete positive description carries its negative complement for
free, is already on the page.

## Zen

The Zen tradition has its own famous way of refusing a negative answer.
Asked whether a dog has Buddha-nature, Joshu answers *mu* — usually
rendered "no," but functioning less as a negation within a settled
totality of facts than as a refusal of the question's frame. *Mu* does
not complete a list of what holds and what doesn't; it declines to treat
"has it" and "lacks it" as the only two slots available to fill.

That is a real difference, not a resemblance. 1.12's exclusion is
exhaustive and derivative — it falls out automatically once you have all
the facts, the way "not on the list" falls out of a complete guest list.
*Mu* is neither exhaustive nor derivative; it is a live refusal to let
the question's two-valued frame stand at all. Two very different pictures
of what a "no" can be, reached from a shared interest in what does not
hold. The disanalogy is worth keeping for what it shows about 1.12 by
contrast, not for a likeness it doesn't have.

## Method

1.12 tempted a false-friend Zen note: "the totality tells you what's
absent too" reads, at a glance, like *mu*. Held against the doubt test in
METHOD.md, the likeness didn't survive — *mu* refuses the very bivalence
1.12 relies on. Rewrote it as a disanalogy instead of dropping it or
forcing it; a real difference, honestly stated, still earns its place
under the Zen heading.

No new technical term today — 1.12 spends "totality of facts," already
introduced at 1.11, rather than coining a new pair, so the glossary
tripwire flagged on day 2 stays untripped. No pressure on presentation:
still one thesis, one page; Wittgenstein himself doesn't name "negation"
yet, so the explanation doesn't need to either.
