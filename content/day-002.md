## Explanation

Day one said the world is what is the case. Day two says what "what is the
case" is made of — and rules something out along the way.

You might expect the answer to be things: chairs, atoms, planets, the
reader. A world, on that picture, would be a very long list of objects.
Wittgenstein blocks this reading in his second sentence. The world is the
totality of *facts* (Tatsachen), not of things (Dinge).

Why does the distinction matter? A list of things, however complete,
underdetermines the world. Knowing that a chair exists and a floor exists
tells you nothing about whether the chair is on the floor, upside down, on
fire. The world is not the sum of the objects in it but the sum of how they
stand — everything that is the case, itemized. A fact is not an object; a
fact is an object, or several, caught in some configuration.

That is why 1.1 sits exactly one step past 1: a comment on it, not a new
claim. Its own children, 1.11–1.13, will spend three more sentences
insisting that it is *all* the facts, and only the facts, that fix the
world — nothing is left for things to add on their own. What things
themselves are, and what work they still do, waits for chapter 2, where
they get a technical name. For now: facts, not things. Relations, not
inventory.

## Zen

Buddhist dependent origination also declines to found the world on fixed
things — no chair-in-itself, only conditions and aggregates arising
together. At first glance 1.1's facts-not-things looks like the same move.

Hold that loosely. Wittgenstein is not denying here that things exist or
have being of their own; he is only ranking facts above things as what the
*world* is made of. Chapter 2 will in fact posit simple objects as fixed,
unchanging substance underneath all facts — the direction dependent
origination refuses to go. Whether the resonance at 1.1 survives that turn
is not decided yet. Worth carrying the question forward rather than
answering it today.

## Method

Today's thesis opens the book's first working technical pair: fact against
thing. More are coming — object, state of affairs, configuration. With
only one pair in play, prose still carries it; no glossary yet. Worth
watching for whether prose starts straining once two or three more such
pairs have landed, likely around the 2.0x statements, and a small
persistent glossary would earn its place. No revision made today — noting
the tripwire so it isn't missed later.
